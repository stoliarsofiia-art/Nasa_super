================================================================================
PHYSICS FORMULAS AND LAWS USED IN EXOPLANET ML SYSTEM
================================================================================

This document lists ALL physics formulas and laws used to generate the 
990 synthetic exoplanets and make predictions.

================================================================================
1. KEPLER'S THIRD LAW OF PLANETARY MOTION
================================================================================

Original Form:
    P² = (4π²/GM) × a³

Where:
    P = orbital period
    G = gravitational constant
    M = mass of star
    a = semi-major axis

Simplified Form (in solar system units):
    a = P^(2/3) × M^(1/3)

Where:
    a = semi-major axis (AU)
    P = orbital period (years)
    M = stellar mass (solar masses)

Used for: Calculating orbital distance from period and stellar mass

Example:
    Kepler-22 b: P = 289.9 days = 0.794 years, M = 0.97 solar masses
    a = 0.794^(2/3) × 0.97^(1/3)
    a = 0.862 × 0.990
    a = 0.853 AU


================================================================================
2. TRANSIT DEPTH FORMULA (GEOMETRY)
================================================================================

Formula:
    δ = (R_planet / R_star)²

Where:
    δ = transit depth (fraction of star's light blocked)
    R_planet = planet radius
    R_star = stellar radius

Physical Basis:
    Transit depth is the ratio of the planet's cross-sectional area to the
    star's cross-sectional area.
    
    Area_planet = π × R_planet²
    Area_star = π × R_star²
    δ = Area_planet / Area_star = (R_planet / R_star)²

Conversion Factor:
    1 solar radius = 109.1 Earth radii

Implementation:
    depth = (planet_radius_earth / (stellar_radius_solar × 109.1))²

Example:
    Planet: 8 Earth radii (Neptune-sized)
    Star: 1.0 solar radii (Sun-like)
    
    depth = (8 / (1.0 × 109.1))²
    depth = (8 / 109.1)²
    depth = 0.0733²
    depth = 0.00537 = 0.537%


================================================================================
3. STELLAR MASS-RADIUS RELATION
================================================================================

Formula:
    R_star = M_star^0.8

Where:
    R_star = stellar radius (solar radii)
    M_star = stellar mass (solar masses)

Physical Basis:
    For main sequence stars, radius scales approximately with mass to the
    power of 0.8. This is an empirical relation from stellar structure theory.

Range of Validity:
    0.5 M☉ < M_star < 2.0 M☉ (K-type to F-type stars)

Example:
    M_star = 1.25 solar masses
    R_star = 1.25^0.8 = 1.19 solar radii


================================================================================
4. STELLAR MASS-LUMINOSITY RELATION
================================================================================

Formula:
    L_star = M_star^3.5

Where:
    L_star = stellar luminosity (solar luminosities)
    M_star = stellar mass (solar masses)

Physical Basis:
    For main sequence stars, luminosity scales approximately with mass to the
    power of 3.5. This comes from stellar structure equations and nuclear
    fusion rates.

Used for: Calculating stellar energy output for planet temperature estimates

Example:
    M_star = 0.97 solar masses
    L_star = 0.97^3.5 = 0.912 solar luminosities


================================================================================
5. PLANET EQUILIBRIUM TEMPERATURE
================================================================================

Full Formula:
    T_eq = T_star × sqrt(R_star / (2×a)) × (1 - A)^0.25

Where:
    T_eq = planet equilibrium temperature (Kelvin)
    T_star = stellar temperature (Kelvin)
    R_star = stellar radius (AU)
    a = semi-major axis (AU)
    A = albedo (reflectivity, 0-1)

Simplified (assuming A ≈ 0.3, R_star ≈ 0.0047 AU for Sun-like stars):
    T_eq = T_star × sqrt(1 / (2×a)) × 0.01

Physical Basis:
    Energy balance: Energy absorbed = Energy radiated
    
    Energy absorbed ∝ L_star / a² × (1 - A)
    Energy radiated ∝ T_planet⁴
    
    Therefore: T_planet ∝ T_star × (L_star/a²)^0.25

Example:
    Kepler-22 b:
    T_star = 5627 K
    a = 0.853 AU
    
    T_eq = 5627 × sqrt(1 / (2×0.853)) × 0.01
    T_eq = 5627 × 0.767 × 0.01
    T_eq = 43.2 K (note: simplified formula gives rough estimate)


================================================================================
6. STEFAN-BOLTZMANN LAW (UNDERLYING TEMPERATURE FORMULA)
================================================================================

Formula:
    L = 4π × R² × σ × T⁴

Where:
    L = luminosity (energy per second)
    R = radius
    σ = Stefan-Boltzmann constant (5.67 × 10⁻⁸ W·m⁻²·K⁻⁴)
    T = temperature (Kelvin)

Used for: Understanding stellar energy emission and planet thermal radiation


================================================================================
7. SIGNAL-TO-NOISE RATIO (SNR) MODEL
================================================================================

Formula:
    SNR = (20 / (1 + P/40)) × e^(-(m-10)/4) × noise

Where:
    SNR = signal-to-noise ratio
    P = orbital period (days)
    m = stellar apparent magnitude
    noise = random factor (0.8 to 1.2)

Component 1 - Period Dependence:
    SNR_period = 20 / (1 + P/40)
    
    Physical Basis: More transits in observation window = better averaging
    Short period (P=2d): SNR_period ≈ 19
    Long period (P=200d): SNR_period ≈ 3.3

Component 2 - Magnitude Dependence:
    SNR_magnitude = e^(-(m-10)/4)
    
    Physical Basis: Brighter stars have less photon noise
    Bright star (m=8): factor ≈ 1.65
    Faint star (m=14): factor ≈ 0.37

Underlying Physics:
    SNR ∝ sqrt(N_transits) × (Signal / Noise)
    
    Where:
        N_transits ∝ T_observation / P
        Noise ∝ 1/sqrt(N_photons) ∝ 10^(m/5)


================================================================================
8. ORBITAL VELOCITY (KEPLER'S SECOND LAW)
================================================================================

Formula:
    v = 2π × a / P

Where:
    v = orbital velocity
    a = semi-major axis
    P = orbital period

Used for: Proxy calculations in feature engineering

Alternative Form (circular orbit):
    v = sqrt(GM / a)

Where:
    G = gravitational constant
    M = stellar mass


================================================================================
9. INSOLATION FLUX (STELLAR IRRADIANCE)
================================================================================

Formula:
    F = L_star / (4π × a²)

Where:
    F = flux received by planet (W/m²)
    L_star = stellar luminosity
    a = semi-major axis

Simplified (relative to Earth):
    F_relative = (L_star / L_sun) / (a / 1 AU)²

Physical Basis:
    Inverse square law - energy spreads over sphere of area 4πa²

Example:
    Venus: a = 0.72 AU, L_star = 1 L_sun
    F = 1 / 0.72² = 1.93 times Earth's insolation


================================================================================
10. TRANSIT DURATION MODEL
================================================================================

Semi-empirical Formula:
    T_duration = 2.0 + 0.5 × ln(1 + P)

Where:
    T_duration = transit duration (hours)
    P = orbital period (days)
    ln = natural logarithm

Physical Basis (complete formula):
    T_duration = (P/π) × sqrt((R_star + R_planet)² - (b×R_star)²) / a
    
    Where:
        b = impact parameter
        
    Simplified because duration scales roughly as P^(1/3) but we use
    logarithmic approximation for computational efficiency.


================================================================================
11. IMPACT PARAMETER
================================================================================

Definition:
    b = (a × cos(i)) / R_star

Where:
    b = impact parameter (0 to 1)
    a = semi-major axis
    i = orbital inclination angle
    R_star = stellar radius

Physical Meaning:
    b = 0: Planet crosses center of stellar disk (central transit)
    b = 1: Planet grazes edge of stellar disk (grazing transit)
    b > 1: No transit (planet misses stellar disk)

For transiting planets:
    0 ≤ b < 1


================================================================================
12. PROBABILITY DISTRIBUTIONS
================================================================================

Lognormal Distribution:
    PDF: f(x) = (1/(x×σ×sqrt(2π))) × e^(-(ln(x)-μ)²/(2σ²))
    
    Used for: Orbital periods, SNR
    Reason: Naturally skewed - many short periods, few long periods

Normal (Gaussian) Distribution:
    PDF: f(x) = (1/(σ×sqrt(2π))) × e^(-(x-μ)²/(2σ²))
    
    Used for: Stellar mass, temperature, magnitude
    Reason: Symmetric around mean (Sun-like stars cluster)

Uniform Distribution:
    PDF: f(x) = 1/(b-a) for x in [a,b]
    
    Used for: Planet radius (within type), impact parameter
    Reason: No preference for specific values in range

13. FEATURE ENGINEERING FORMULAS
================================================================================

Duration-to-Period Ratio:
    ratio = T_duration / P
    
    Physical meaning: Fraction of orbit spent transiting

Radius Ratio:
    R_ratio = sqrt(transit_depth)
    
    Physical meaning: R_planet / R_star

Estimated Impact Parameter (from observables):
    b_estimate = 1 - (duration_period_ratio / (2 × sqrt(transit_depth)))
    
    Derived from transit geometry

Signal Strength:
    signal = SNR × sqrt(transit_depth)
    
    Physical meaning: Detection significance × signal amplitude

Stellar Density Proxy:
    ρ_proxy = M_star / (T_star/5778)⁴
    
    Based on mass-radius-temperature relations

Semi-major Axis Estimate:
    a_estimate = (P/365.25)^(2/3) × M_star^(1/3)
    
    From Kepler's Third Law

Equilibrium Temperature Estimate:
    T_eq_estimate = T_star × sqrt(R_star / (2×a))
    
    From energy balance

Multiple Event Statistic (MES) Proxy:
    MES = SNR × sqrt(N_transits)
    MES ≈ SNR × sqrt(T_observation / P)
    
    Physical meaning: Combined significance of all transits

Transit Probability:
    P_transit = R_star / a
    
    Physical meaning: Geometric probability of alignment

Depth-to-Noise Ratio:
    DNR = transit_depth × SNR
    
    Physical meaning: How well depth is measured

14. CLASSIFICATION CORRECTION FORMULAS
================================================================================

Confirmation Score:
    score = weighted_sum of:
        - SNR (0-40 → 0-30 points)
        - Transit depth (0-0.05 → 0-20 points)
        - Period (0-500 → 0-15 points)
        - Stellar characterization (0-15 points)
        - Duration consistency (0-10 points)
        - Physical plausibility (0-10 points)
    
    Total: 0-100 points

Probability Adjustment:
    If score ≥ 90:
        Target: 92% confirmed, 4% candidate, 4% false
    If score ≥ 80:
        Target: 85% confirmed, 8% candidate, 7% false
    If score ≥ 70:
        Target: 75% confirmed, 15% candidate, 10% false
    
    Blend: 90% target + 10% original model probabilities


================================================================================
15. UNCERTAINTY METRICS
================================================================================

Shannon Entropy:
    H = -Σ(p_i × log(p_i))
    
    Where:
        p_i = probability of class i
        Sum over all 3 classes

Normalized Uncertainty:
    U = H / log(3)
    
    Range: 0 (certain) to 1 (maximum uncertainty)

Model Agreement:
    agreement = fraction of models predicting same class

Prediction Variance (for properties):
    variance = std(predictions from multiple models)
